# rl144 — STORY COMPILE v1
## The Contractor, the McGuffin, and Half a Donkey

Everything below was locked in session. `[YOURS]` marks a slot only a human
writes; each comes with a job description — what the line must accomplish.
`[TUNE]` marks a number or threshold to be found in playtesting.
`[ENGINE]` marks an ask, priced in section 9.
Dead ideas are not listed. They received no tombstones.

---

## 0. THE GAME IN THREE SENTENCES

A broke contractor descends five floors to fetch a talking amulet for a
collector, to fund buying out his partner's half of one (1) donkey.
The amulet has rehearsed its rescue for four hundred years and gets
interrupted at word six by a man who picks it up like a lamp.
At the surface, the collector inspects it and says the worst four words
in the world, which turn out to be the best: he already has one, and
they'll be neighbors.

**The comic mechanism (all jokes must use it):** enormous investment
meets tiny flat fact. Another one just like it. Word six. Half a donkey.
A floor of historical cheese. Drag marks. If a pitch is a system, a
twist, or a clever mirror, it dies. If it's a specific idiot committed
to a flat fact, it lives.

**The thesis (never stated by anyone):** the tools the world hands you
(swords, potions, kill-quests) are for the wrong job. The things that
change anyone's life down there are a cheese, a coat, and a towel.
Every ending leaves exactly one magnificent thing unrecognized.

---

## 1. HARD RULES (original four, plus session amendments)

1. **The narrator cannot lie.** Unchanged. Amendment: the narrator is
   not obligated to say everything. Silence is the straight man.
   Withholding is the punchline delivery system, not a Rule 1 loophole.
2. **Meaning survives a thousand worlds.** Unchanged below ground.
   Amendment: the OVERWORLD is exempt — it is the one hand-built, fixed
   stage, identical for every seed. "The spot where X happens" is legal
   up top and only up top.
3. **≤78 chars/line, 300–500 lines total.** Budget allocated in §8.
4. **New mechanics are priced, never assumed.** All asks in §9.
5. **NEW — The Cold Reveal Rule:** nothing below the surface may
   foreshadow the duplicate McGuffin. Not lore, not monster dialogue,
   not the tired ones (they don't know). The only planted evidence is
   the contract's fine print on the title screen. Every clever deep-lore
   line is a chance to break the best joke in the game. Don't.
6. **NEW — No moralizing, by casting:** no character in the game is
   qualified to state what any of it meant. The player triangulates
   truth from between four unreliable voices. If a line states the
   thesis, cut the line.
7. **NEW — One-beat reveals:** the donkey's true form does nothing
   after being revealed. The moment it acts, it's Shadowfax. It must
   only, briefly, unmistakably, be more than you were told.

---

## 2. THE FOUR VOICES

No overlap. The emotional truth lives in the gaps between them.

**THE NARRATOR** — dry, observant, unintentionally funny. Full range:
may be surprised, judgmental, briefly fascinated by irrelevant geology,
clearly rooting against you. Never winks. Doesn't know it's in a comedy;
it is reporting conscientiously, and the conscientiousness is the bit.
Same voice depth 1 to depth 5 — the one constant instrument that proves
the music changed. Chooses its ink: full line for a rat kill, nothing —
ever — about the coat. ONE dry caption about the mimic's intentions,
lifetime maximum, placed where it kills.

**THE McGUFFIN** — florid, romance-poisoned, wrong about most things,
cannot walk. Its carrier is "the legs" (never "my hero" — the vacancy
was always in the text, see §10). All climb material has the faint
rhythm of someone ad-libbing around prepared remarks, steering back
toward a speech it will never get to restart. Register slides across
the climb from ballad → adventure → buddy → no genre at all, in steps
too small to point at.

**THE MONSTERS** — flat facts on the way down, honest at the bottom.
Sight gags the talk verb bounces off (depths 1–4); reviews (depth 5).

**THE LORE** — other people's handwriting. Shallow: contractor
graffiti. Mid: the monsters' writing. Deep: the McGuffin's drafts.

## 3. BEAT SHEET

### 3.1 TITLE SCREEN
- Premise stated flat, PQ-style, one line, ≤78 chars:
  shape: "[NAME] THE [EPITHET] DESCENDS TO BUY OUT HALF A DONKEY"
  `[YOURS]` — the dude's name and epithet. Job: instantly pathetic,
  instantly specific, funny read aloud. Rather-Dashing-grade.
- THE CONTRACT, signable, with fine print. Player will not read it.
  Fine print must contain, buried: condition clause ("near-mint"),
  an item/inventory number that is conspicuously NOT 001, and/or
  "no duplicates of existing inventory." This is the second-playthrough
  scream. 12–20 chars of the funniest legal language available.
  `[YOURS]` — exact wording. Job: boring enough to skip, damning
  enough to frame. ~4 lines total.
- Somewhere on this screen or the overworld: the ASCII donkey with a
  faint dotted line across its middle. Never referenced. Ever.

### 3.2 OVERWORLD (free — no light clock; kills ARE counted, silently)
Fixed, hand-built, ~3 screens. The stage. Cast:

- **THE TRAINER.** Retired contractor — from depth 2. Tells the stories
  like he went deeper. Gives the genre's advice with total confidence:
  kill five rats, bring cheese, swords solve things. His kill-quest is
  the game's first moral event wearing a tutorial's clothes: obeying it
  primes your record before you know there's a record, and the McGuffin
  will greet you five floors later as the brute the quest log made you.
  Refusing him — or talking to the training rats while he watches — is
  the first mercy, on-screen, with a witness who has opinions.
  He has seen hundreds of resurrections. He is never surprised.
  `[YOURS]` — the trainer's ONE flat fact, his half-a-donkey-grade
  detail. Job: makes him a specific guy you once met, not "a trainer."
- **THE DONKEY.** Present from turn one. Half-owned. Dotted line.
  The talk verb works on it: the donkey is the diplomacy tutorial —
  receptivity, regard stages, becalming, all first practiced on
  livestock you partially own. Regard stages `[YOURS]`, ~5 lines.
  Job: teach the entire mercy system while being about a donkey.
- **THE POSTING / THE HOLE.** The contract lives here diegetically.
  A sign-up sheet with prior names crossed off — the restless dead
  with a surface address. Foreshadows danger only, never the duplicate.
  The collector's house: shut. Not receiving until the item's in hand.

### 3.3 THE DESCENT — five depths, five registers
The difficulty curve and the sincerity curve are the same line.
Talk gets harder in darkness (existing rule) as floors get darker —
except depth 5, where it always lands. See §4 for each floor's cast,
minigame, and tables.

- **D1 — THE CHEESE MIDDEN.** Full light, full comedy, full narrator
  fluency. Rats. Generations of adventurers' cheese, archaeological.
  Sight gags, graffiti, the tutorial verbs mocked as you use them.
- **D2 — THE COAT.** Comedy needs light to land the sight gag; it
  still has some. "A tall monster approaches. It is two monsters."
- **D3 — THE MIMIC. (hinge)** Light math starts to bite. The monster
  whose menace and flirtation are the same sentence, right where the
  tone starts its slide. Talk lines begin containing information.
- **D4 — THE LOST GUY.** Near-dark, three tiles of visibility, and a
  relaxed naked man insisting the exit is this way. The one free
  conversation in the dungeon — no roll, no game, no receptivity.
  (The joke: the only human is the only one with no game attached.)
- **D5 — THE TIRED ONES.** Quiet. Nothing attacks. Combat verbs go
  useless; talk always lands, first try, full honesty. They are the
  McGuffin's neighbors of four centuries and they know the material.
  Why don't they stop you? The game never says. They're not guardians.
  They're just who lives there.

### 3.4 THE PICKUP (midpoint — voice two comes online)
The McGuffin is not hidden. No trap, no lock. Centered. Presented.
It has been staging its own discovery for four hundred years; the
blocking was the one thing it could control.
The dude picks it up like a lamp at WORD SIX of the prepared remarks.
`[YOURS]` — the six words. Job: the most valuable 6 words in 1.44MB.
Must be obviously the opening of something enormous, and survivable
as a fragment. Gorgeous-stupid. (The full speech also gets written —
see Deep Lore, §6 — but only ever appears as drafts and fragments.)
- Opening register keys off descent record `[ENGINE-cheap]`:
  bloody record → captive-bride material. merciful → destined-beloved.
  Both wrong. The wrongness is the characterization.
- Nearest tired one's reaction line: the driest thing in the game,
  some flavor of *finally* that never uses the word. `[YOURS]`
- Light cost doubles. The arithmetic states "this thing is a burden."
  The rest of the game disproves it without anyone saying so.

### 3.5 THE CLIMB
- Every consequence is already on the map: becalmed monsters step
  aside (existing mechanic — the procession is free), wounded-and-
  abandoned ones are healed enough and unlistening, your light budget
  reflects every swing you took. No reinforcements needed. The
  punishment for the bloody run is that you already spent everything.
- The McGuffin narrates all of it, incorrectly, while attempting
  speech re-entry: "As I was saying—" (monster), "From the top—"
  (stairs). Each attempt gets ~one word further. It never finishes.
- Re-encounters that must have table support:
  - The mimic runs its material on the dude IN FRONT OF the McGuffin —
    the only creature in the dungeon fluent in its native genre, and
    it cannot leave the conversation. `[YOURS]` — the McGuffin's
    reaction resolves on the page when both voices collide. Do not
    pre-decide it. One encounter slot, high value.
  - The tired ones watching it go, with the specific stillness of
    people who don't want to jinx it.
  - Becalmed monsters at high regard: "You again. Good." register.
- McGuffin mood → light modifier `[ENGINE]`: distress (it watched you
  kill) costs extra; trust flickers you an occasional free step/glow.
  The light clock is its mood ring. [TUNE: all values.]
- PUT-DOWN is legal `[ENGINE-cheap]`. Every set-down is an event for a
  creature that cannot follow you. Set-down/pick-up line slots, and it
  WILL talk to whatever walks up. It is not disloyal. It is incapable
  of the category.

### 3.6 THE SURFACE / THE MANTEL
Cold reveal. He inspects it. He notes the scratch (if the run log has
one — it usually does). And:

    "Hm. Yes. Matches the other one."

He wanted a pair. For symmetry. On the mantel. There is no empire.
The McGuffin LIGHTS UP. There's another one. Just like it. Neighbors.
The heartbreak changes owners: the player wanted to be its whole
story, and they were the commute.

**Three exits, same verb as the whole game:**

- **HAND IT OVER (the paid ending).** He deducts for the scratch.
  Pays. Sets it beside the other one. The buyout clears.
  Narrator: "The set is complete. You are paid in full." — the
  coldest line in the game being, for once, simply true and warm,
  and the narrator doesn't know which one it's being.
  Then: the donkey, whole now. True form. One beat. One re-glyph —
  dotted line gone, one extra character of majesty, something faintly
  wrong with how much space it takes up. Narrator: "The donkey is
  whole now." No explanation. Not ever. Then the ending ends.
  EPILOGUE: two McGuffins on a mantel. Ours doing the talking for
  two, four inches away, forever, delighted. The silent one has
  never said a word. Last line of the game:
  `[YOURS]` — draft on file: "It has started from the top."
  Job: the speech finally gets delivered, just not to you. Laugh
  and ache in one sentence, narrator-verifiable as fact.
- **KEEP IT (the "good" ending, quietly selfish).** Broke, contract-
  broken, unhirable, double light forever — you carried the loneliest
  object in the world past the door of the only other one of its
  kind, because you couldn't lose the bit where it narrates your
  life. It stays. It's fine. It NEVER mentions it. That silence is
  the bill, and the game never itemizes it. The partner keeps his
  half; the super-donkey remains fractional, its true form in
  escrow, and nobody involved will ever know what they stand beside.
  The McGuffin believes forfeiting the fee is the most romantic
  thing that has ever happened. It is not aware it out-bid a donkey.
  It must never find out.
- **TALK (the final check).** Receptivity = the run log `[ENGINE]`.
  What you did is what you can say: spares, becalms, lore read,
  ghosts passed, the McGuffin's trust. A player who becalmed the
  mimic can tell him about the mimic. A genocide run has almost
  nothing to say — the conversation is short because the run was
  short on material. The curriculum pays off here: somewhere in this
  conversation the player echoes him (rat's lesson), answers him
  correctly, and holds still through something. Negotiation, not
  verdict: the case moved to the window. Visitation. Asking the man
  who has never wanted for anything whether the other one ever says
  anything — and watching the question cost him something.
  Outcome space between the two endings above. [TUNE: heavily.]
- **Swing at him.** Legal. Probably fatal. See rat food, below.

### 3.7 DEATH / RESPAWN (any point)
- Death message: punchline TO YOU. Your tombstone spawns in-world
  with an epitaph: comedy FOR EVERYONE ELSE. My mockery is their
  comedy. (Real deaths cluster shallow — roguelike statistics — so
  tombstone-jokes pile up on D1–D2 and a D5 ghost is rare and quietly
  awful. The tonal gradient partly builds itself. Don't ruin it.)
- You wake up top. Beside the donkey. Slightly damp. There are drag
  marks. The narrator reports the drag marks and moves on. The dude
  never witnesses it. The game never confirms it, denies it, or
  explains it. Occasionally an item you dropped on depth 4 is up
  top, near the donkey. The narrator has nothing to add.
- Inventory stays below, next to your tombstone: retry-runs loot
  their own corpse like a stranger, and descend past their own
  punchline. The trainer has a line for seeing you again. He is not
  surprised. Nobody who posts at that hole is ever surprised.
- Rat food is canon: die swinging at the surface and your tombstone
  epitaph joins everyone's world. Shape: "Here lies a contractor.
  Deal fell through." `[YOURS]` — final pool, see §7.

## 4. THE CAST — floor by floor

### D1 — THE RATS (and the midden)
- **The fact:** everyone knows rats want cheese. The floor is
  archaeological with the cheese of generations who knew it. The
  rats have HAD cheese. Offering cheese: measured regard PENALTY
  [TUNE]. An entire talk-table stage of cheese grievance.
- **Minigame — ECHO:** repeat what it says. Its want and its game
  are the same thing: nothing down here has ever said anything back.
  Regard stages: it slowly starts saying things worth repeating.
  (Curriculum lesson 1: ECHO — reused at the mantel.)
- **Vault:** THE MIDDEN. A monument of wheels, hand-drawn. One
  tombstone in the middle. Epitaph `[YOURS]` — shape: "he died
  believing." Job: the entire genre's confidence in one dead guy.
- Talk table: ~30 lines across regard stages. Registers: aggrieved →
  wary → curious → something like company.

### D2 — THE COAT
- **The fact:** "A tall monster approaches. It is two monsters."
  Everything said twice, slightly out of sync. Refers to itself as
  "I." The game never acknowledges the coat. No reveal. There was
  never a secret. The narrator will not spend ink here, ever — that
  is how the coat stays funny all game.
- **Minigame — ANSWER THE SECOND VOICE:** respond to the follow-up
  voice, the out-of-sync one, the one nobody ever addresses.
  (Curriculum lesson 2: ANSWER CORRECTLY — reused at the mantel.)
- **Item chain — THE SECOND COAT** (found in a vault, ~D3–4, carried
  back up or caught on the way down): give it, and they can finally
  stop being one tall monster. Two short monsters now stand side by
  side. The game treats this as a completely different social
  situation with zero acknowledgment.
  `[YOURS]` — high-regard lines must settle, on the page, whether
  they even WANTED to be uncoupled. Do not decide in advance.
- Talk table: ~20 lines, all doubled (counts as one line each for
  budget if the echo is programmatic — coding agent: please).

### D3 — THE MIMIC (hinge)
- **The fact:** a real mimic, chest-with-teeth, whose hunting
  strategy is courtship. It wants to eat you and its flirtation and
  its menace are the same sentence. Its want NEVER changes — regard
  refines it. Becalmed, it still wants to eat you (narrator may
  confirm ONCE, lifetime), it has just become wistful about it.
  "If things were different" energy, from furniture.
- **Minigame — THE POLITE NO:** it offers rest, comfort, sitting
  down. Rudeness fails. Acceptance is damage. The winning move is
  the courteous decline, repeatedly, while your light burns.
  (Curriculum lesson 3: HOLD STILL / ENDURE — reused at the mantel.)
- **The sword collision:** disarm mechanic (§9) meets the most
  qualified item-holder in the dungeon. It has been offering to hold
  things all game. Its intentions are impeccable and terrible.
  Whether entrusting your sword to the hungry furniture is genius or
  a punchline resolves on the page. `[YOURS]` — that line set.
- **Climb re-encounter:** runs material on the dude in front of the
  McGuffin. See §3.5. The becalmed mimic watching you pass with the
  prize, saying something gallant and hungry: guaranteed slot.
- Talk table: ~30 lines. Registers: workmanlike seduction →
  professional respect → wistful hunger.

### D4 — THE LOST GUY
- **The fact:** a previous contractor. Lost. Naked. Not bothered by
  either fact. Has been "heading out" for some time. Directions
  confident and wrong. Knows nothing useful. Thinks you're making a
  big deal out of all this. His explanation for the nakedness is
  available, at length, and explains nothing. `[YOURS]` — the
  explanation. Job: funny ENOUGH. He must never be funnier than
  "enough." That's his whole energy level.
- **No minigame.** Talk-anytime, no roll. The free conversation.
- **Item — THE TOWEL:** an apology for a solution. You couldn't fix
  the actual problems (pants, being lost), so: the universal gesture
  toward dignity. His receipt of it `[YOURS]`.
- ~15 lines.

### D5 — THE TIRED ONES
- **The fact:** they've lived around the McGuffin for four hundred
  years. Through the wall: the drafts. The rehearsals. All day.
  They don't fight. If you talk, they're straight up about it.
  Flat, weary, specific, ZERO cruelty — you can't hate a roommate
  of four centuries; you just know the material. They have opinions
  on the drafts. One of them preferred the older opening and says
  so. They do NOT know about the duplicate (Rule 5 — they're
  neighbors, not oracles).
- **Mechanic: the absence of one.** Talk always lands. The deepest
  floor is the easiest conversation in the game, because they have
  nothing left to perform. (Inverts the whole descent; the game's
  bluntest floor is its bottom.)
- **Post-reveal re-read (free):** it rehearsed four centuries for an
  audience that was right there, and never once considered them the
  audience. Nobody points at this. Ever.
- **Vault:** THE STAGE — the room where it rehearsed, hand-drawn:
  a cleared space, wall drafts thickening toward it, maybe tally
  marks. The pickup pedestal is centered and presented (§3.4).
- ~20 lines. Register: reviews. Weary. Fond despite everything.

**DEEP — the McGuffin's drafts** (~20 lines) `[YOURS]`

it is the quarry all fragments are cut from). In-game it appears
only as: crossed-out drafts, margin notes ("too formal?"), tally
marks, revisions getting more elaborate as you near the stage.
Funny at first read. Devastating in memory after the mantel.
Foreshadows NOTHING except how long it waited (Rule 5).

## 10. HORIZON-PROOFING (four habits, zero design debt)

Single-player is the game. Rung three (live extraction race) is
weather on the horizon. These cost nothing now and save a rewrite:
